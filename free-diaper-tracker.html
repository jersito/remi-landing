Red Flags

When to call the pediatrician

Most diaper questions are normal. These six are worth a same-day call.

💧

Fewer wets than expected

In a newborn: fewer than 6 heavy wets per day after day 5. In older babies: a sudden drop from their normal count. Both can be early signs of dehydration or a feeding problem.

🩸

Blood in stool

A streak of red or black in the diaper always gets a call. Sometimes it is a small anal fissure from a hard stool. Sometimes it is a milk protein reaction or something that needs a closer look.

💦

Persistent watery diarrhea

Three or more watery, explosive diapers in a row, especially with a fever or less feeding, can dehydrate a baby fast. Call the same day.

🥤

Dehydration signs

Dry mouth, no tears when crying, a sunken soft spot, fewer wet diapers, and a sleepy or limp baby together. Do not wait on this one.

⚪

White, grey, or chalky stool

Pale, putty-colored stool at any age can point to a liver or bile duct issue. Get it checked.

🟢

Unusual color plus other symptoms

Bright green frothy stools in a fussy baby can mean a foremilk-hindmilk imbalance. Black stool after day 5 (not meconium) or jelly-like red stool always get a call.

Guide

How to use a diaper log (and why it matters)

In the first weeks, diaper counts are one of the clearest signals you have that your baby is feeding well. Before weight gain shows up on the scale, and before milestones tell you anything, the diaper tells the truth. That is why every pediatrician asks about wets and dirties at the early visits. A diaper tracker just makes that conversation easy.

Why wet diapers matter

Heavy, pale-yellow wet diapers mean your baby is getting enough milk. A rough target is one wet diaper per day of life until day 5, then at least 6 heavy wets per day for the first month. If wets are dropping or look dark and concentrated, your baby may be under-fed or dehydrated. Track them and share the count with your pediatrician if something feels off.

Why dirty diapers matter

Stool shows you how your baby is digesting. In the first week, you will see the full color progression: black meconium on day 1 and 2, greenish brown on day 3, yellow and seedy by day 4 to 5 in breastfed babies. Formula-fed babies have tan or yellow-brown stool that is a bit firmer. After 6 weeks, breastfed babies sometimes go days between dirty diapers and that can be completely normal as long as the stool stays soft when it comes.

Breastfed vs. formula-fed differences

  • Breastfed stool is yellow, seedy, and loose. Often with every feed in the early weeks, then much less frequent after 6 weeks.
  • Formula-fed stool is tan to yellow-brown, firmer, and less frequent. Usually 1 to 4 per day.
  • Combination feeding falls somewhere between. Expect color and texture to shift as the balance shifts.
  • After solids start (around 6 months), stool firms up, changes color, and starts to smell more adult. Totally normal.
