Tummy time goals, month by month

The AAP recommends working up to 15 to 30 minutes a day by 7 weeks and about 60 minutes a day by 4 months. Spread it across several short sessions. Short and happy beats long and crying every single time.

Ideas that actually work

Tummy time ideas for babies who hate tummy time

Almost every baby protests at first. It is new, it is hard, and gravity is rude. These small tweaks turn crying sessions into something your baby will actually tolerate.

🫂

Chest-to-chest

Lie back on the couch or bed and lay your baby tummy-down on your chest. You are the view, the warmth, and the anchor. Counts as tummy time from day one.

🧻

Rolled towel or nursing pillow

Tuck a rolled receiving blanket or a firm pillow under your baby's armpits and chest. The slight lift takes the work down a notch and helps them see more of the world.

🪞

Unbreakable baby mirror

Prop a baby-safe mirror right at face level. Babies are wired to stare at faces, even their own. Mirror time easily adds 60 to 90 seconds to any session.

🎪

Play gym, upside down

Flip a play gym sideways or use a low arch so dangling toys sit in front of your baby's face instead of above. Gives them something to track and reach for.

🦵

Tummy-down over your lap

Sit down, lay baby across your thighs tummy-down, and gently pat their back. Great for a fussy baby who will not tolerate the floor yet. Bonus: helps with gas.

🐶

Sibling, pet, or window view

Set up tummy time facing a big sibling playing, the family dog, or a sunny window with moving leaves. Novelty keeps their head up longer than any toy.

Guide

Why tummy time matters, and how to start

Tummy time is the awake, supervised time your baby spends on their belly. It is one of the simplest, most evidence-backed things you can do for your baby's physical development, and it takes exactly zero equipment. The problem is that most newborns genuinely do not like it at first, so parents quietly give up. That is where a timer and a log help. When you can see that you already got 12 minutes in today across four tiny sessions, the whole thing feels winnable.

What tummy time actually does

Three things, mostly. It builds the neck, shoulder, and upper back muscles your baby needs for rolling, sitting, and crawling. It keeps pressure off the back of the skull, which lowers the risk of plagiocephaly, the flat spot that can form when babies spend a lot of time on their backs. And it gives your baby a new view of the world, which is surprisingly important for visual tracking and motor planning. The reason safe-sleep guidelines ask babies to sleep on their backs is that it saves lives, but that also means awake belly time has to do the muscle-building work that used to happen passively.

How to start with a newborn

You can start tummy time on day one. In the first weeks, the easiest version is chest-to-chest on you while you are reclined. That counts. Aim for a minute or two, several times a day, working up to about 15 minutes a day by the end of the first month.

  • Pick a moment when your baby is awake and calm, not hungry and not right after a feed. Right after a diaper change is perfect.
  • Use a firm, flat surface. A play mat or a blanket on the floor is ideal. Never a couch, bed, or any soft bedding.
  • Get your face down at their face level. You are the most motivating toy they have.
  • Start the timer for a realistic goal, not an aspirational one. Two minutes is a win.
  • Stop before the meltdown. You want the association to be "this is okay", not "this is torture".

Fussy baby troubleshooting

If your baby cries within 30 seconds every single time, the setup usually needs a tweak, not more willpower. Try chest-to-chest first for a week. Add a rolled towel under the armpits to take the load off. Put a mirror or a high-contrast black-and-white card right in front of their face. Do it right after a diaper change when they are fresh. And stack little sessions through the day instead of one long one. Three minutes, six times a day, gets you to 18 minutes without a single tear.

When to contact your pediatrician

  • Your baby cannot lift their head at all by 4 months old.
  • One side of the head looks flatter than the other, or your baby strongly prefers turning their head one direction (a sign of torticollis, which is very treatable).
  • Persistent asymmetry: one arm or leg moves noticeably less than the other.
  • Your baby's head feels heavy and their neck seems to have no strength by 3 months.
  • You are worried. Gut feeling counts. Call.

Plagiocephaly (flat head syndrome) and torticollis (tight neck muscles) are both common and both very fixable when caught early, usually with more tummy time, repositioning tricks, and sometimes a short course of physical therapy. Your pediatrician will know what to do, and you will not be the first parent to ask.
