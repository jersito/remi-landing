How Often to Bathe a Newborn: A Gentle Guide

Pippy Team April 9, 2026 7 min read
Disclaimer: This article is for informational purposes only and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always consult your pediatrician or healthcare provider with questions about your baby's health.

If you are holding a tiny, sleepy newborn and wondering whether it is bath night yet, you are not alone. Figuring out how often to bathe a newborn is one of those small questions that can feel surprisingly big, especially in the first few weeks when everything is new. The short answer is that babies do not need as many baths as you might think, and a gentle, low key approach is often the kindest choice for their skin.

In this guide, we will walk through how often most pediatricians suggest bathing a newborn, when to start a full tub bath, how to make the experience soothing, and simple signs that it may be time to check in with your doctor. You are doing a wonderful job already, and bath time does not need to be perfect to be good enough.

How Often to Bathe a Newborn

Most pediatricians suggest bathing a newborn about two to three times a week during the first year. That may feel surprisingly infrequent if you pictured nightly bubble baths, but newborns simply do not get very dirty. They are not crawling in the grass or eating pasta with their hands yet. The parts of their body that actually need regular cleaning, like the face, neck folds, hands, and diaper area, can be freshened up with a warm washcloth any time.

Bathing more often than that is not wrong, especially if your baby loves the water and it helps your family wind down. But newborn skin is thin and still developing, and frequent baths, especially with soap, can strip away the natural oils that keep it soft and protected. Many parents find that two to three baths a week, paired with gentle daily wipe downs, keeps their little one clean and comfortable without drying out their skin.

If your baby has a medical condition, very sensitive skin, or a recommendation from their doctor to bathe more or less often, follow that guidance first. Every baby is different, and your pediatrician knows your baby's specific needs.

Sponge Baths vs. Tub Baths

In the first days and weeks, sponge baths are the go to. A sponge bath is simply a gentle wipe down with a warm, damp washcloth while your baby lies on a soft towel in a warm room. There is no tub involved, which keeps the umbilical cord stump dry while it heals. If you want a refresher on caring for that little belly button area, our guide on newborn umbilical cord care walks through the basics.

Many parents stick with sponge baths until the cord stump has fallen off and the belly button area looks fully healed, which usually happens within one to three weeks. After that, a gentle tub bath is generally fine. Some families love baby bathtubs that sit inside a larger tub or on a counter, while others use the kitchen sink with a padded insert. There is no single right setup. Pick what feels safe, supported, and comfortable for you.

When you transition to tub baths, keep the water shallow, usually around two to three inches, and always keep one hand on your baby. Newborns are slippery when wet, and their heads are heavy for their little necks, so steady support is the priority.

Building a Gentle Bath Routine

Bath time can be a lovely little anchor in your day, even when everything else feels unpredictable. You do not need a long list of products or a perfectly decorated nursery. A warm room, a soft towel, a gentle cleanser if you choose to use one, and your calm presence are really all you need. Many parents find that a bath works beautifully as part of a bedtime routine for babies, helping signal that the day is winding down.

Keep baths short, around five to ten minutes, so your baby does not get chilled. Warm up the room beforehand, have everything you need within arm's reach, and sing, hum, or narrate softly while you wash. Babies love hearing your voice, and it helps them feel safe in a new experience. If your baby is fussy in the bath, try again another day. Some babies may warm up to the water slowly, and that is completely normal.

After the bath, wrap your baby in a hooded towel right away and pat, do not rub, them dry. Pay special attention to the folds under the chin, behind the ears, in the armpits, and around the thighs, where moisture can linger.

Bath Safety Basics

Bath safety comes down to a few simple habits that quickly become second nature. Water should be warm, not hot. The general guidance is around 100 degrees Fahrenheit, or about 38 degrees Celsius. Test the water with the inside of your wrist or elbow before lowering your baby in. If it feels hot to you, it is too hot for your baby.

Never leave your baby alone in the bath, not even for a second, and not even in a tiny amount of water. If the doorbell rings or the phone buzzes, scoop your baby up and bring them with you wrapped in a towel. Bath seats and rings can give a false sense of security, so they should not replace hands on supervision.

Gather everything you need before starting. A towel, washcloth, fresh diaper, clean clothes, and any gentle cleanser should be within arm's reach. Keeping everything close means you never have to take your eyes or your hands off your baby.

Newborn Skin Care Between Baths

Because you are not bathing your newborn every day, gentle between bath care matters. Wipe the face, neck folds, and hands with a warm, damp washcloth as needed, and keep the diaper area clean and dry at each change. A fresh diaper and a quick wipe can prevent a lot of irritation.

Newborn skin is famously flaky and often shows little rashes or bumps in the first weeks. Most of these are harmless and pass on their own. If you notice scaly patches on the scalp, our post on cradle cap basics is a good place to start. For dry, itchy patches on the body, baby eczema basics can help you understand what you are seeing. Some babies may benefit from a fragrance free moisturizer after baths, but check with your pediatrician before using new products.

Keep an eye on the diaper area too. If you notice persistent redness, clean gently with water, let the skin air dry when you can, and ask your doctor about the right care if it does not improve.

When to Call Your Pediatrician

Baths are usually a low stress part of the day, but sometimes skin or bath time issues are worth a quick call to your doctor. If you notice spreading redness, blisters, peeling skin beyond normal newborn flakiness, or a rash that does not fade, it is a good idea to reach out. The same goes for any sign of infection around the belly button, like warmth, swelling, or oozing, especially while the cord stump is still healing.

If your baby seems unusually uncomfortable during or after baths, or if bath time is suddenly much harder than it used to be, trust your instincts and check in with your pediatrician. You know your baby best, and there is no question too small to ask.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should you bathe a newborn?
Most pediatricians suggest bathing a newborn about two to three times a week during the first year. Daily baths are usually not needed and can dry out a baby's skin. Between baths, a gentle wipe of the face, neck, hands, and diaper area is usually enough. If you have any concerns about your baby's skin, talk to your pediatrician.
When can I give my newborn their first real bath?
Many parents stick with sponge baths until the umbilical cord stump has fallen off and the area has fully healed, which usually happens within one to three weeks. After that, a gentle tub bath is generally fine. Always check with your pediatrician if you are unsure when to transition.
Is it okay to bathe a newborn every day?
Daily baths are usually not necessary for a newborn and can leave their skin feeling dry. If your baby enjoys the water and it is part of a calming routine, a short daily bath can be fine as long as you moisturize afterward. Talk to your pediatrician if you notice dryness, flaking, or irritation.
What time of day is best to bathe a newborn?
There is no single best time. Many parents find that a warm bath in the evening helps their baby wind down before sleep, while others prefer mornings when their baby is calm and alert. Every baby is different, so pick the time that works best for your routine.
How warm should the bath water be for a newborn?
The general guidance is to keep bath water around 100 degrees Fahrenheit, or about 38 degrees Celsius. Always test the water with your wrist or elbow before placing your baby in, and never leave your baby unattended in the bath, even for a second.
Do I need to use soap every bath?
Soap is not needed at every bath. Many parents find that plain warm water is enough for most baths, with a mild, fragrance free baby wash used a couple of times a week. If your baby has sensitive skin or you notice any irritation, ask your pediatrician for product suggestions.
