How to Take a Baby's Temperature: A Safe, Simple Guide

Pippy Team April 16, 2026 8 min read
Disclaimer: This article is for informational purposes only and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always consult your pediatrician or healthcare provider with questions about your baby's health.

If you have ever stood in the middle of the night with a warm, fussy baby and a thermometer in your hand, wondering which end goes where and whether that reading is actually right, you are not alone. Learning how to take a baby's temperature is one of those skills nobody really teaches you, and it suddenly feels very important the moment you need it.

The good news is that with the right thermometer, a little bit of technique, and a calm approach, you can get an accurate reading pretty quickly. In this guide, we will walk through the different ways to check a baby's temperature, how to pick the best method for your baby's age, and what the numbers actually mean. Most importantly, we will talk about when it is time to pick up the phone and call your pediatrician.

Every baby is different, and what works smoothly for one family might be tricky for another. That is okay. Use this as a starting point, and lean on your pediatrician for anything that feels uncertain.

Why Accurate Temperature Readings Matter

In babies, especially newborns, a temperature reading is more than just a number. It is a small window into how your baby's body is handling the world around them. A fever in a very young baby can be an early sign of an infection that needs prompt attention, which is why pediatricians take fevers in infants under 3 months very seriously.

At the same time, a single reading that is off by a degree can change the picture. That is why technique really matters. A rushed armpit reading over thick pajamas can make a real fever look like nothing, and a rectal reading done right after a warm bath can make a normal temperature look high. Taking a moment to set things up properly helps you trust the number you see.

It also helps to know that babies naturally run a little warmer than adults. Their temperature regulation is still developing, and things like being bundled up, active crying, or a warm room can nudge a reading upward. This is one reason your pediatrician will often ask about the context, not just the number itself.

How to Choose the Right Thermometer

Walk down any pharmacy aisle and you will find shelves of baby thermometers, each promising to be the easiest, fastest, or most accurate. The truth is that most modern digital thermometers are quite good, and the best one for your family is often the one you feel comfortable using. For newborns, a simple digital thermometer that can be used rectally is the workhorse most pediatricians recommend.

Forehead (temporal artery) thermometers have become very popular because they are quick and gentle. They can be a helpful screening tool at any age, especially when your baby is sleeping. Some babies may squirm less with a forehead scan than with a rectal reading. That said, results can be affected by sweat, hair, or a cold room, so many parents find it useful to keep a digital thermometer on hand as a backup.

Ear (tympanic) thermometers are usually best for babies over 6 months, since newborn ear canals are tiny and curved. Older-style glass thermometers with mercury should not be used at all, since they can break and release a toxic metal. If you still have one tucked in a medicine cabinet from years ago, most pharmacies can help you dispose of it safely.

Temperature Methods by Age

The best method for your baby depends largely on how old they are. Younger babies need more accurate readings, while older babies have more options. The table below summarizes what most pediatricians suggest, though your own doctor may have a specific preference based on your baby's history.

Age Recommended Method Also Okay Generally Avoid
0 to 3 months Rectal Armpit (axillary) for quick screening Ear, oral
3 to 6 months Rectal or forehead (temporal) Armpit for screening Ear, oral
6 months to 3 years Rectal, forehead, or ear Armpit for screening Oral (until your child can safely hold the thermometer under their tongue)
3 years and older Oral, forehead, or ear Armpit for screening Rectal is rarely needed

General guidance based on common pediatric recommendations. Always check with your own pediatrician about what they prefer for your baby.

If you have a young baby, do not worry if a rectal reading feels intimidating. Most pediatricians or nurses are happy to show you the technique at a well visit. Many parents find it much less stressful once they have watched it done once and tried it with a little guidance.

Step by Step: How to Take Each Type of Reading

Rectal temperature (most accurate for babies under 3 months)

Lay your baby on their back with their knees bent up toward their chest, or on their tummy across your lap. Put a small dab of petroleum jelly on the silver tip of a clean digital thermometer. Gently insert the tip about half an inch to one inch into the rectum, stopping as soon as you meet any resistance. Hold the thermometer in place with one hand and steady your baby with the other until it beeps. Clean the thermometer thoroughly after use, and label it so it is never mixed up with an oral one.

Armpit (axillary) temperature

Undress your baby so that their armpit is bare (clothing between the sensor and skin can throw things off). Tuck the tip of the thermometer into the center of the armpit and gently hold your baby's arm down against their side so the thermometer stays snug against skin. Wait for the beep. Armpit readings are usually about a degree lower than a rectal reading, so they are a good screening tool but not the final word.

Forehead (temporal artery) temperature

Make sure your baby's forehead is dry and not flushed from being bundled up. Follow the instructions for your specific thermometer, which usually involves sweeping across the forehead or holding the device a few inches away. If your baby was just sleeping on that side or has a sweaty forehead, try the other side or gently pat the skin dry first. If a reading seems surprisingly high or low, take a second reading to confirm.

Ear (tympanic) temperature (6 months and older)

Gently pull your baby's outer ear back and slightly up to straighten the ear canal, then insert the thermometer tip snugly. Keep it still until you hear the beep. Earwax, a cold ear from being outside, or ear infections can all affect readings, which is why this method works best as a quick check in older babies rather than the go-to for newborns.

Reading the Numbers: Normal vs. Fever

Once you have a reading, the next question is what it means. Normal ranges vary a little depending on the method and the baby, but here is a general guide many families find helpful. As always, talk to your pediatrician about what counts as a fever for your specific baby.

Method Typical Normal Range Generally Considered a Fever
Rectal 97.9 to 100.4 °F (36.6 to 38.0 °C) 100.4 °F (38.0 °C) or higher
Armpit (axillary) 97.0 to 99.0 °F (36.1 to 37.2 °C) 99.0 °F (37.2 °C) or higher
Forehead (temporal) 97.5 to 100.1 °F (36.4 to 37.8 °C) 100.4 °F (38.0 °C) or higher
Ear (tympanic, 6+ months) 97.0 to 100.0 °F (36.1 to 37.8 °C) 100.4 °F (38.0 °C) or higher

General reference ranges. Your pediatrician can help interpret readings based on your baby's age, medical history, and other symptoms.

It can also help to know that babies' temperatures naturally ebb and flow through the day. Many babies run slightly cooler in the early morning and a bit warmer in the late afternoon and evening. A single reading of 99.4 degrees Fahrenheit on an armpit check in a warmly bundled baby who just woke from a nap is rarely a reason to panic, though it is always okay to recheck in a cooler outfit.

When to Call Your Pediatrician

Pippy says:

When in doubt, call. Pediatric offices truly expect calls about fevers, and you are never bothering them by checking.

For babies under 3 months old, any rectal temperature of 100.4 degrees Fahrenheit or higher should prompt a same-day call to your pediatrician or, if their office is closed, a visit to urgent care or the emergency department. Young infants cannot fight off infections as well as older babies, so doctors prefer to evaluate them quickly, even if everything turns out to be fine.

For babies 3 to 6 months old, call if the fever reaches 102 degrees Fahrenheit, lasts more than 24 hours, or comes with symptoms that concern you, such as unusual sleepiness, poor feeding, persistent crying, fewer wet diapers, or a rash. For a deeper look at fever and when it becomes urgent, see our guide on baby fever and when to call the doctor.

For older babies and toddlers, behavior often tells you more than the number. A baby with a 101 degree fever who is drinking well, smiling between cuddles, and playing a bit is generally less worrying than a baby with 99.5 degrees who is listless and refusing the breast or bottle. Always trust your gut. If something feels off, your pediatrician is there to help. Fever with other symptoms like poor feeding, vomiting, or signs of dehydration deserves extra attention. You can learn more about what to watch for in our piece on signs of dehydration in babies.

Keeping Your Baby Comfortable During the Check

Taking a temperature tends to go best when both you and your baby are calm. Some parents find it helpful to change a diaper or wash up beforehand, not because it is strictly necessary, but because getting into a routine can make the whole thing feel less like a medical event. A familiar soft toy, gentle singing, or a warm, calm voice can go a long way.

If you are taking a rectal reading, warming the thermometer tip briefly in your hand can make the moment more comfortable. Move slowly, talk softly, and keep the insertion very shallow. It is completely fine to stop, cuddle, and try again in a minute if your baby is really upset. A short pause usually results in a more accurate reading anyway, since crying can bump the temperature up.

For forehead or ear readings, position matters more than you might think. Try to catch your baby during a quiet moment, such as the end of a feed or during a drowsy cuddle. Some babies may resist having anything near their face, and that is normal. Playing with a thermometer (with it turned off) during calm times can help your baby get used to seeing and hearing it before they ever need one.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most accurate way to take a baby's temperature?
Most pediatricians consider a rectal temperature to be the most accurate for babies under 3 months. It gives the closest reading to your baby's true core temperature, which matters a lot in young infants. For older babies, an armpit reading or a good forehead thermometer can be helpful for a quick check, and a rectal reading can be used to confirm if needed. Always talk to your pediatrician about what they recommend for your baby's age.
Is it safe to take a baby's rectal temperature at home?
Yes, rectal temperatures are generally considered safe when done gently and with a dedicated rectal thermometer. You only need to insert the tip about half an inch to one inch, never force it, and keep one hand on your baby at all times. Many parents find it helpful to have a pediatrician or nurse walk them through the steps once so they feel more confident. If anything feels off, stop and call your doctor.
What counts as a fever in a baby?
A rectal temperature of 100.4 degrees Fahrenheit (38 degrees Celsius) or higher is generally considered a fever in babies. For armpit readings, a fever is usually considered 99 degrees Fahrenheit or higher, and forehead readings fall somewhere in between depending on the device. Any fever in a baby under 3 months old should prompt a call to your pediatrician right away, even if your baby seems otherwise comfortable.
How old does my baby need to be for a forehead thermometer?
Forehead (temporal artery) thermometers can be used at any age as a quick screening tool, but many pediatricians suggest confirming with a rectal reading for babies under 3 months. Some newer forehead and ear thermometers work well in older babies and toddlers, though technique and positioning really matter. If you are ever unsure about a reading, take it again or use a different method and check with your pediatrician.
Can I use an ear thermometer on a newborn?
Most pediatricians do not recommend ear (tympanic) thermometers for babies under 6 months. Newborn ear canals are very small and curved, which can make readings less reliable. For the youngest babies, a rectal thermometer is typically the most accurate option, and an armpit check can work for a quick read. Once your baby is 6 months or older, an ear thermometer can be a helpful tool when used correctly.
Do I need to call the pediatrician every time my baby has a fever?
For babies under 3 months, yes, any fever of 100.4 degrees Fahrenheit or higher deserves a same-day call. For older babies, you do not always need to call right away if your baby is still eating, drinking, and acting like themselves, but you should reach out if the fever lasts more than a day or two, keeps climbing, or comes with other worrying symptoms. When in doubt, always call your pediatrician.
