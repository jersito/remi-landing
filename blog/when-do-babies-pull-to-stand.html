When Do Babies Pull to Stand? What to Expect

Remi Team April 4, 2026 7 min read
Disclaimer: This article is for informational purposes only and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always consult your pediatrician or healthcare provider with questions about your baby's health.

One day your baby is sitting on the floor, perfectly happy to play with whatever is in arm's reach. The next, they have spotted the edge of the couch and they are absolutely determined to get up there. That moment when babies pull to stand for the first time is one of those milestones that tends to catch parents off guard, and then completely melt their hearts.

It is also one of the milestones that tends to bring a lot of questions. When exactly does this happen? What if my baby does it earlier than expected, or later? Should I be helping them practice? And how do I keep them from bonking their head every five minutes? If any of those questions sound familiar, you are in the right place.

This guide covers everything parents generally want to know about the pulling-to-stand milestone, from the typical age range to safety tips to what comes next. Keep in mind that every baby develops at their own pace, and the ranges here are meant to give you a general picture, not a strict deadline.

When Do Babies Typically Pull to Stand?

Most babies pull to stand somewhere between 8 and 10 months of age. That said, the full typical range runs from about 7 months on the early end to 12 months on the later end, and both are completely within the range of normal development. Some babies are very motivated movers who want to be upright as soon as possible. Others are more deliberate and take their time working through each step before they attempt the next one.

If your baby has already been sitting independently and is crawling or scooting around, pulling to stand is usually the next big gross motor leap on the horizon. You may notice them starting to grab at low surfaces, testing whether things can hold their weight, even before they make their first real pull-up attempt. That exploratory phase is a great sign that the milestone is approaching.

It is also worth noting that babies who spent extra time in the NICU, were born prematurely, or have certain developmental differences may reach this milestone on a slightly different timeline. If you have any questions about your specific baby's development, talking to your pediatrician is always the best move.

Age Range Gross Motor Skill What It Looks Like
4 to 6 months Sitting with support Baby can sit when propped up; head control is steady
6 to 8 months Sitting independently Baby sits on their own without needing hands for balance
6 to 10 months Crawling or scooting Baby moves across the floor; style varies widely
7 to 12 months Pulling to stand Baby uses furniture or a person's hands to pull up to standing
9 to 12 months Cruising Baby walks sideways while holding onto furniture
9 to 15 months First independent steps Baby lets go and takes steps without holding on

Every baby is different. These ranges are general guidelines based on typical development. Talk to your pediatrician if you have concerns about your baby's progress.

The Stages Leading Up to It

Pulling to stand does not happen out of nowhere. It is the result of months of your baby building up core strength, leg strength, and coordination through earlier milestones. Understanding the stepping stones can help you see where your baby is in the process and what might come next.

It usually starts with sitting independently, which gives babies the core stability they need to control their body in an upright position. From there, most babies start to get mobile through crawling, scooting, or some other form of floor movement that strengthens their arms, legs, and hips. Once a baby can reliably move around and has figured out how to bear weight on their legs when held standing, they start to put those skills together.

Many parents notice a kind of pre-pulling phase where the baby kneels next to a surface, places both hands on it, and just sort of hangs out there for a while. That is a very normal first step. From kneeling, most babies figure out how to push up with their legs to get upright. The first few attempts often involve a lot of wobbling, a surprised look on their face, and more than a few gentle sit-downs back to the floor.

How to Help Your Baby Practice

The good news is that you do not need any special equipment to help your baby work toward this milestone. The most helpful thing you can do is create an environment with safe, low, sturdy surfaces that give them something to grab onto. A couch cushion on the floor, a low ottoman, or a padded activity table are all popular choices. Many parents find that placing a favorite toy on a slightly elevated surface gives babies all the motivation they need to start reaching upward.

Tummy time continues to matter at this stage too. If your baby is still in the earlier phases of motor development, regular tummy time helps build the upper body and core strength that feeds into pulling up. Even a few minutes at a time throughout the day adds up.

You can also hold your baby in a supported standing position during play. Letting them feel what it is like to bear weight on their legs, even with your help, gives them a preview of what standing feels like and helps those leg muscles get stronger. Just follow their lead. If they want to sit back down, let them. Forcing the position or holding them up for extended periods is not necessary and may frustrate them.

Keeping Baby Safe When They Start Pulling Up

Once your baby discovers they can pull to stand, they will want to do it everywhere, on everything, at all hours of the day. This is when baby-proofing really becomes a top priority if you have not tackled it yet.

One of the most important steps is anchoring heavy furniture to the wall. Bookshelves, dressers, and TV stands can tip over if a baby grabs them and pulls. Wall anchors are inexpensive, widely available, and genuinely important at this stage. It is also a good time to lower your baby's crib mattress to the lowest setting if you have not already, since a baby who can pull to stand can also potentially pull themselves up over a crib rail.

Add corner guards to sharp furniture edges, move anything breakable or hazardous off low shelves and tables, and take a low-to-the-ground look around your home to see it from your baby's perspective. Soft play mats or rugs near common practice spots can help cushion the inevitable tumbles, because there will be tumbles. Most are completely harmless, but having a soft landing spot nearby gives both you and your baby a little extra cushion.

Falls at this stage are normal and expected. Some babies are cautious and lower themselves back down carefully from the start. Others just let go and drop, and they seem totally unbothered by it. Most babies figure out how to sit back down safely within a few weeks of mastering the pull-up.

What Comes After Pulling to Stand?

Once your baby has pulling to stand figured out, the next stop on the gross motor journey is usually cruising. Cruising is when babies walk sideways while holding onto furniture, moving along the couch, circling around the coffee table, and generally exploring as much of the room as their grip can take them. It is one of the cutest phases, and it is also genuinely important preparation for independent walking.

From cruising, many babies start to experiment with letting go for a second or two. They might stand unsupported, look extremely proud of themselves, and then grab right back on. Those moments of independent standing gradually get longer until one day they take a step without holding on. Most babies take their first independent steps somewhere between 9 and 12 months, and most are walking confidently by around 15 months.

The timeline from pulling to stand to walking varies quite a bit from baby to baby. Some babies spend just a few weeks cruising before they strike out on their own. Others cruise for several months and seem in no particular hurry to let go. Both approaches are normal.

When to Check In With Your Pediatrician

Most babies reach the pulling-to-stand milestone without any concerns, but there are a few situations where it is worth bringing up at a doctor's visit. If your baby is not pulling to stand by around 12 months, especially if they also seem to avoid putting weight on their legs or show little interest in moving around, that is worth mentioning to your pediatrician. They can take a closer look and let you know whether any follow-up is needed.

It is also worth noting any asymmetry in how your baby moves. If they consistently favor one side over the other, for example always pulling up with one arm or not bearing weight evenly on both legs, that is something a pediatrician would want to know about.

Your regular well-child visits are a great opportunity to check in on motor development. Many parents find it helpful to keep notes or track milestones in the weeks leading up to an appointment so they can give their doctor a clearer picture of what they have been observing at home.
