Newborn Umbilical Cord Care: What to Know and When to Call Your Doctor

Pippy Team April 5, 2026 6 min read
Disclaimer: This article is for informational purposes only and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always consult your pediatrician or healthcare provider with questions about your baby's health.

Bringing your baby home from the hospital is one of the most exciting (and nerve-wracking) moments of early parenthood. And right there on your newborn's tiny tummy is a little reminder of the incredible journey you both just went through: the umbilical cord stump. If you're feeling unsure about how to care for it, you're definitely not alone. Many new parents feel a bit anxious about this small but important part of newborn care.

The good news? Caring for your baby's umbilical cord stump is simpler than you might think. In most cases, the best approach is to keep it clean, keep it dry, and let nature do its thing. This guide walks you through what to expect, how to care for the stump day by day, and the signs that mean it's time to give your pediatrician a call.

Every baby is a little different, so what's normal for one newborn may look slightly different for another. When in doubt, your baby's doctor is always the best person to ask.

What Is the Umbilical Cord Stump?

After your baby is born, the umbilical cord is clamped and cut. What remains is a small stump, usually about one to two inches long, that stays attached to your baby's belly button. This stump is made up of tissue that no longer has blood flowing through it, so over the coming days and weeks it will naturally dry out, shrink, and eventually fall off on its own.

When you first see it, the stump may look yellowish-green and feel slightly moist. As it dries, it will darken in color and become firmer. This is completely normal and a sign that the healing process is happening just as it should. Many parents are surprised by how the stump changes appearance over the first week or two, but these changes are typically nothing to worry about.

The area underneath the stump is essentially a small wound that needs time to heal. Once the stump falls off, you may notice a small raw spot that will continue to heal over the next few days. Think of it as your baby's very first "boo-boo," and it usually resolves quickly with simple, gentle care.

How to Care for the Cord Stump

The general guidance from most pediatricians today is surprisingly simple: keep it clean and keep it dry. Years ago, parents were often told to swab the stump with rubbing alcohol at every diaper change. However, more recent research suggests that leaving the stump alone and letting it dry naturally may actually help it fall off faster. Your pediatrician can let you know their specific recommendation for your baby.

When it comes to bathing, most healthcare providers suggest sticking with sponge baths until the cord stump has fallen off and the belly button area looks fully healed. This means using a warm, damp washcloth to gently clean your baby rather than submerging them in water. It's a great time to focus on those adorable little folds and creases while keeping the stump area dry. If you're looking for more tips on those early days at home, our guide on surviving the first week with a newborn covers the basics.

If the stump does get a bit dirty, perhaps from a diaper leak, you can gently clean around the base with a damp cotton swab or soft cloth. Pat the area dry afterward rather than rubbing, and let it air out as much as possible. Loose, comfortable clothing that doesn't press tightly against the stump is ideal during this time.

What to Expect as the Stump Heals

In the first few days, the stump will look soft and may have a yellowish or greenish tint. This is perfectly normal. Over the course of the first week, it will begin to dry out and darken, sometimes turning brown or even black. While this can look a little alarming, it's actually a sign that the stump is healing properly and getting ready to separate.

As the stump gets closer to falling off, you might notice it hanging on by just a thin thread of tissue. It can be tempting to give it a gentle tug, but it's best to resist that urge and let it come off naturally. Pulling the stump before it's ready could cause unnecessary bleeding or discomfort for your baby.

Once the stump does fall off, you may see a small amount of blood or a slightly raw area where it was attached. Some parents also notice a small amount of clear or slightly yellowish fluid. This is normal and usually heals within a few days. If you want to keep track of how the healing progresses, logging a note in Pippy can be a helpful way to remember what you observed and when.

Cord Stump Healing Timeline

While every baby heals at their own pace, here's a general timeline that many parents can expect. Keep in mind that some babies are on the faster side and others take a bit longer, and both are usually perfectly fine.

TimeframeWhat You May Notice
Days 1 to 3Stump is soft, yellowish-green, and moist. The clamp may still be attached.
Days 3 to 7Stump begins to dry and may darken in color. Clamp is usually removed by your provider or falls off.
Days 7 to 14Stump is dry, dark brown or black, and noticeably smaller. May start to loosen at the base.
Days 10 to 21Stump falls off on its own. A small raw spot or minor bleeding at the belly button is common.
Days 21 to 28Belly button area continues to heal and dry. Should look clean and dry within a few days of the stump separating.

These are general ranges. If your baby's stump hasn't fallen off by 4 weeks, talk to your pediatrician.

Diapering Around the Cord Stump

Diaper changes happen constantly with a newborn, and navigating around that little cord stump adds an extra layer of care. The key is to keep the diaper from covering or rubbing against the stump. Many newborn-sized diapers come with a small notch cut out at the front specifically for this purpose, which can be really helpful.

If your diapers don't have a cord notch, you can simply fold the front of the diaper down below the stump. This keeps the area exposed to air and reduces the chance of irritation from moisture or friction. It also makes it easier to spot any changes in the stump's appearance during changes. For more on mastering diaper changes in those early weeks, check out our post on how often to change your baby's diaper.

Some parents find it helpful to let their baby have a bit of "tummy air time" each day, where the stump area is uncovered for a few minutes. This can speed up the drying process and give you a chance to check on how things look. Just make sure the room is warm enough so your little one stays comfortable.

When to Call Your Pediatrician

Most of the time, the umbilical cord stump heals without any issues at all. But because it is essentially an open area on your baby's body, it's important to know the signs that something might need medical attention. Trust your instincts here. If something doesn't look or feel right to you, it's always okay to call your baby's doctor.

Some signs that are worth reaching out to your pediatrician about include redness or swelling around the base of the stump, a foul-smelling discharge (not just a mild odor), bleeding that doesn't stop with gentle pressure, or your baby seeming to be in pain when the area is touched. A fever in a newborn, even a low one, should always be reported to your doctor promptly. Our post on baby fever and when to call the doctor has more details on what to watch for.

Another thing to mention at your next well-baby visit: if the stump hasn't fallen off after about four weeks, your pediatrician may want to take a closer look. In rare cases, a small piece of moist tissue called an umbilical granuloma can form after the stump separates. This is usually easy to treat, but it's something your doctor should evaluate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take for a newborn's umbilical cord stump to fall off?
Most umbilical cord stumps fall off on their own within 1 to 3 weeks after birth. Some may take a bit longer, and that is usually perfectly normal. If the stump has not fallen off after 4 weeks, it is a good idea to mention it to your pediatrician.
Should I clean my baby's umbilical cord stump?
The current guidance from most pediatricians is to keep the cord stump clean and dry. If it gets soiled, you can gently clean around the base with a damp cotton swab and let it air dry. Alcohol swabs are generally no longer recommended, but check with your baby's doctor for their specific advice.
Can I give my newborn a bath before the cord falls off?
Most healthcare providers recommend sponge baths rather than tub baths until the cord stump falls off and the area heals. This helps keep the stump dry and supports the natural healing process. Talk to your pediatrician about when it is safe to start tub baths.
Is it normal for the umbilical cord stump to smell?
A mild odor can be normal as the stump dries and separates. However, a strong or foul smell, especially combined with redness, swelling, or discharge, could be a sign of infection. If you notice these symptoms together, contact your pediatrician right away.
What should I do if the cord stump bleeds a little?
A small amount of bleeding or spotting when the stump separates is common and usually not a cause for concern. Gently dab the area with a clean cloth and keep it dry. If the bleeding does not stop or seems heavy, reach out to your pediatrician for guidance.
